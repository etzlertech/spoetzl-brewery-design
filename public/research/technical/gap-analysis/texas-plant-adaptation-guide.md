# Texas Plant Adaptation Guide
## Adapting Busch Gardens Williamsburg Landscape Design for Shiner, Texas Climate

---

## Executive Summary

This guide provides comprehensive plant substitution recommendations for adapting Busch Gardens Williamsburg's intensive landscape design approach to Shiner, Texas (USDA Zone 9a). The analysis addresses climate differences, water conservation requirements, and provides specific cultivar recommendations with botanical names and maintenance protocols.

**Climate Context:**
- **Shiner, Texas:** Zone 9a (20°F to 25°F minimum), hot summers (95°F typical high), 43°F winter lows
- **Williamsburg, VA:** Zone 7b-8a, cooler summers, higher humidity, more consistent rainfall
- **Key Challenges:** Extended heat (rarely above 100°F but sustained 95°F+), water conservation needs, alkaline soils

## 1. SPRING PROGRAM ADAPTATIONS

### 1.1 Tulip Alternatives (BGW: 50,000 tulips)

**Challenge:** Tulips require 8-10 weeks of pre-chilling in Zone 9a and must be treated as annuals.

#### **Tulips in Texas: Feasibility Assessment**

**Pre-Chilling Requirements:**
- Zone 9a requires 8-10 weeks refrigeration at 35-45°F
- Plant in January after chilling (mid-November start)
- Remove all fruit from refrigerator (ethylene gas kills flower buds)
- Must be treated as annuals; cannot naturalize

**Cost-Benefit Analysis:**
Pre-chilling 50,000 tulip bulbs requires significant refrigeration infrastructure and labor. **Recommendation: Partial use (5,000-10,000 bulbs for high-visibility European theme areas) + native alternatives.**

#### **Texas Spring Alternatives to Tulips**

| BGW Plant | Texas Alternative | Botanical Name | Visual Similarity | Water Needs | Planting Time |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|-------------------|-------------|---------------|
| Tulips (mixed) | **Texas Bluebonnets** | *Lupinus texensis* | Vertical spike form, vibrant blue | Very Low | Sept-Dec (bloom March-May) |
| Tulips (red) | **Indian Paintbrush** | *Castilleja indivisa* | Red/orange vertical color | Very Low | Sept-Nov (bloom March-May) |
| Tulips (yellow) | **Pre-chilled Daffodils** | *Narcissus* spp. | Similar structure, naturalize better | Low | Nov-Dec (bloom Feb-April) |
| Tulips (white) | **White Larkspur** | *Consolida ajacis* 'White' | Vertical spike, cottage garden feel | Low | Sept-Nov (bloom March-May) |
| Tulips (purple) | **Purple Phlox** | *Phlox drummondii* | Mass color, fragrant | Low-Moderate | Sept-Oct (bloom March-May) |

**Special Note on Bluebonnets:**
- Plant seeds September 1 - December 15 (October optimal)
- Scarify seeds to increase germination
- Cover seeds only 1/4 inch deep
- Water lightly every 3 days for 3 weeks if no rain
- Requires alkaline, well-drained soil (perfect for Texas)
- Develop strong roots over winter for abundant spring flowers

### 2.3 Heat-Tolerant Tropical Alternatives

#### **Hibiscus in Texas**

**Performance:** Excellent. Both tropical and hardy varieties thrive in Texas heat.

**Recommended Varieties:**
- **Tropical Hibiscus** (*Hibiscus rosa-sinensis*): 6-8 hours full sun, large blooms, treat as tender perennial (Zone 9a borderline hardy)
- **Texas Star Hibiscus** (*Hibiscus coccineus*): Native, hardier, red star-shaped flowers, highly heat/humidity tolerant
- **Luna Series Hardy Hibiscus** (*Hibiscus moscheutos*): Dinner-plate flowers (8-10"), fully hardy Zone 5-9

**Maintenance:** Full sun, moderate water (established plants tolerate brief drought), attracts hummingbirds.

#### **Lantana: Native vs. Non-Native**

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION:**

| Type | Species | Status | Recommendation |
|------|---------|--------|----------------|
| **Texas Lantana** (RECOMMENDED) | *Lantana urticoides* | Native, non-invasive | Orange flowers, drought-proof, heat-proof |
| **Tropical Lantana** (AVOID) | *Lantana camara* | Non-native, invasive | Pink/yellow, spreads aggressively |

**Use ONLY:** *Lantana urticoides* (native Texas lantana) or non-invasive cultivars. Provides continuous bloom until fall, attracts butterflies/hummingbirds, extremely drought tolerant.

**Alternative Non-Invasive Options:**
- Mealy Blue Sage (*Salvia farinacea*)
- Gregg's Mistflower (*Conoclinium greggii*)
- Butterfly Weed (*Asclepias tuberosa*)

## 3. FALL PROGRAM ADAPTATIONS

### 3.1 Chrysanthemums in Texas Heat

**Feasibility:** YES, with cultivar selection and timing adjustments.

**Challenge:** Many chrysanthemum cultivars experience delayed or reduced flowering under high temperatures (26-32°C / 79-90°F). Texas heat extends into September/October.

#### **Heat-Tolerant Chrysanthemum Cultivars**

Texas A&M Bexar County Extension recommends these cultivars for San Antonio area (similar to Shiner climate):

| Color | Recommended Cultivars | Notes |
|-------|-----------------------|-------|
| **White** | Encore, Illusion, Nicole, Tolima | Clean white, heat-stable |
| **Yellow** | Donna, Goldmine, Jessica, Target | Bright gold tones |
| **Pink** | Debonair, Naomi, Stardom, Sundoro | True pink in heat |
| **Purple/Lavender** | Barbara Lavender, Ursula Lavender, Venice Purple, Beth Purple | Rich tones |
| **Dark Pink/Purple** | Amor Dark Pink | Deep color saturation |
| **Bronze** | Ginger, Grace, Sandy, Triumph | Fall-appropriate tones |
| **Red** | Bravo, Minngopher, Remarkable | True red, no fading |

**Growing Requirements:**
- **Timing:** Plant in early September for October/November bloom
- **Temperature:** Prefer 60-70°F for budding; tolerate Texas conditions once established
- **Hardiness:** Zone 3-9 (Shiner is Zone 9a)
- **Soil:** Rich, humusy, well-drained, pH 6.5-6.7
- **Water:** Moderate; established plants are drought-tolerant
- **Sun:** Full sun (6+ hours)

**Management Strategy:**
- Select cultivars with proven heat tolerance
- Plant early September to allow acclimation before peak bloom
- Provide afternoon shade if temperatures exceed 85°F in September
- Mulch heavily to moderate soil temperature

### 4.1 Texas Alternatives to 400-Year-Old Oaks

**BGW Context:** Ancient oaks (primarily *Quercus virginiana* varieties and *Quercus alba*) provide cathedral-like structure and historical gravitas.

**Texas Equivalent Analysis:**

#### **Live Oak Species Comparison**

| Characteristic | Virginia/Southern Live Oak (*Q. virginiana*) | Texas Live Oak (*Q. fusiformis*) | Escarpment Live Oak (*Q. fusiformis* var.) |
|----------------|---------------------------------------------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Native Range** | East TX to Virginia coast | Central/South Texas | Texas Hill Country (west) |
| **Hardiness** | Zone 8-10 | Zone 8-10 | Zone 7-9 |
| **Growth Rate** | 2-3 ft/year (young) | 2-3 ft/year (young) | 2-3 ft/year (young) |
| **Mature Height** | 40-80' | 40-50' | 35-45' |
| **Mature Spread** | 60-120' | 60-100' | 50-80' |
| **Trunk Diameter** | Slower to thicken | **Faster trunk girth** | **Fastest trunk girth** |
| **Drought Tolerance** | Moderate | High | **Very High** |
| **Alkaline Soil** | Tolerates | Prefers | **Thrives** |
| **Leaf Retention** | Semi-evergreen | Semi-evergreen | Semi-evergreen |

**Key Insight:** Texas varieties (*Q. fusiformis*) add trunk girth **faster** than Virginia varieties despite similar height growth. For creating "instant age" appearance, Texas Live Oak is superior.

**Recommendation for Shiner:**
- **Primary:** Texas Live Oak (*Quercus fusiformis*) - optimal for Central Texas, faster mature appearance
- **Secondary:** Escarpment Live Oak - superior drought tolerance for xeric zones
- **Spacing:** 60-80' centers for mature spread (same as BGW)

**Expected Timeline:**
- Year 1-10: Rapid growth (2-3 ft/year)
- Year 10-20: Slowing growth, canopy development
- Year 20-40: Mature character developing
- Year 40+: "Ancient" appearance (with proper trunk caliper selection at install)

**Source:** [Live Oak Species Comparison - Oaktopia](https://www.oaktopia.org/species/texaslive)

## 6. WATER-WISE STRATEGIES

### 6.1 Drip Irrigation Recommendations

**Efficiency Comparison:**
- **Spray Irrigation:** ~65% efficient (BGW likely uses this)
- **Drip Irrigation:** **90% efficient** (properly designed/maintained)
- **Water Savings:** Up to 70% less water than traditional sprinklers

#### **System Design for Texas Landscapes**

**Zone Configuration:**

| Zone Type | Irrigation Method | Emitter Type | Frequency (Summer) | Notes |
|-----------|------------------|--------------|-------------------|-------|
| **Annual Beds** | Drip tape / inline emitters | 0.5-1.0 GPH, 12" spacing | Daily (15-20 min) | Shallow roots |
| **Perennial Beds** | Drip line / bubblers | 1.0-2.0 GPH, 18-24" spacing | 2-3x weekly | Deeper roots |
| **Trees (establishing)** | Bubblers / soaker ring | 2-4 GPH per tree | 1-2x weekly (deep soak) | Years 1-3 |
| **Trees (established)** | Bubblers (optional) | 2-4 GPH per tree | Monthly OR rain-only | Years 4+ |
| **Shrubs** | Drip line / emitters | 1.0 GPH, 18" spacing | 2x weekly | Moderate needs |
| **Groundcovers** | Drip tape | 0.5 GPH, 12" spacing | 2-3x weekly | Establishing phase |
| **Turf (if any)** | Separate spray system | N/A - rotary nozzles | 1-2x weekly (per restrictions) | Minimize turf |

**Best Practices:**
- **Zoning by Water Needs:** Group plants with similar requirements (hydrozoning)
- **Median/Groundcover Areas:** Drip irrigation only (TCEQ compliance, avoids pavement overspray)
- **Pressure Regulation:** Use pressure regulators (10-25 PSI for drip)
- **Filtration:** Required for drip systems (prevent clogging)
- **Backflow Prevention:** Required by code

**Smart Controllers:**
- Weather-based (adjust for rain, temperature, humidity)
- Soil moisture sensors (prevent overwatering)
- May qualify for rebates from local water utilities

**Drought Restriction Exemptions:**
- In some Texas municipalities, drip irrigation is **exempt from drought restrictions**
- Verify with Shiner/Lavaca County water authority

**Source References:**
- [Drip Irrigation Best Practices - TxDOT](https://www.txdot.gov/manuals/des/lad/chapter-3--landscape-development/section-5--landscape-irrigation.html)
- [Texas Water Conservation Irrigation - TCEQ](https://www.tceq.texas.gov/drinkingwater/irrigation/irr_helpres.html)

### 6.2 Mulching Strategies for Texas Heat

**Critical Function:** Mulch reduces soil temperature and evaporation—essential for Texas conditions.

#### **Mulch Benefits (Quantified)**

| Benefit | Impact | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| **Evaporation Reduction** | 50-70% reduction vs. bare soil | LSU AgCenter |
| **Soil Cooling** | Prevents rapid soil heating from sun | Urban Oasis |
| **Moisture Retention** | Absorbs water, gradually releases | Grass Works Austin |
| **Temperature Stabilization** | Moderates fluctuations | Various |

#### **Mulch Type Selection for Texas**

| Mulch Type | Depth | Best Use | Pros | Cons |
|------------|-------|----------|------|------|
| **Texas Native Hardwood Mulch** | 2-4" | Annual/perennial beds | Moisture retention, organic matter | Decomposes (annual refresh) |
| **Shredded Cedar** | 2-3" | Beds, pathways | Aromatic, insect-repellent | Lighter (blows in wind) |
| **Pine Bark Nuggets** | 2-3" | Shrub beds, trees | Long-lasting, attractive | Can float in heavy rain |
| **Cypress Mulch** | 2-3" | Wet areas, general beds | Moisture retention, slow decomp | More expensive |
| **Compost** | 1-2" | As soil amendment (under other mulch) | Adds nutrients, improves soil | Not sole mulch (too fine) |
| **Gravel/Stone** | 2-3" | Xeric gardens, succulents, Mediterranean | Permanent, excellent drainage | Heats up (use for heat-lovers only) |
| **Shredded Leaves** | 2-4" | Native beds, informal areas | Free, adds organic matter | Blows away, decomposes quickly |

#### **Application Guidelines**

**Depth:**
- **Ideal:** 2-4 inches
- **Too Thick:** >4" reduces oxygen to soil
- **Too Thin:** <2" allows weeds, insufficient insulation

**Technique:**
- **Sloping Mound:** Slope away from plant stems/trunks (prevents rot)
- **Keep Clear:** 2-3" gap around stems/trunks (air circulation)
- **Even Distribution:** Consistent depth across bed
- **Edge Definition:** Mulch to bed edge (suppresses grass intrusion)

**Timing:**
- **Primary Application:** Early spring (before heat onset, April)
- **Refresh:** Fall (September-October) - add 1-2" to restore depth
- **Trees:** Mulch year-round (permanent mulch rings)

**Special Considerations for Texas:**
- **Heat Reflection:** Avoid light-colored stone mulches near plants (reflect heat upward)
- **Organic vs. Inorganic:** Organic mulches cool better but require annual refresh; inorganic (gravel) for permanent xeric plantings
- **Mulch + Drip Irrigation:** Place drip lines UNDER mulch (protects from UV, hides from view)

**Source References:**

### 8.2 Heat Management in Texas Greenhouses

**Challenge:** Texas summers (June-September) reach 95-100°F+ ambient. Greenhouses can exceed 120°F without cooling.

#### **Cooling System Recommendations**

| Cooling Method | Temperature Reduction | Cost | Best Application | Notes |
|----------------|---------------------|------|------------------|-------|
| **Evaporative Cooling (Wet Wall)** | 10-20°F | High (install) / Low (operate) | Large commercial greenhouses | Most effective in dry climates; less effective in humid TX |
| **Exhaust Fans + Shutters** | 5-15°F | Medium | All greenhouse sizes | Creates airflow, removes hot air |
| **Misting/Fogging Systems** | 5-10°F | Medium | Medium-large greenhouses | Micro-mist evaporates quickly, cools air |
| **Shade Cloth (50-70%)** | 10-15°F | Low | All greenhouse sizes | Reduces light intensity, critical for TX |
| **Automated Vent Systems** | 5-10°F | Medium | All sizes | Opens/closes based on temperature |
| **Combination Systems** | 20-30°F | High | Commercial scale | Wet wall + fans + shade = best results |

**Recommended System for Shiner:**
- **Wet Wall Evaporative Cooling** on one end
- **Exhaust Fans** on opposite end (creates linear airflow)
- **Automated Shade Cloth** (deploys at 85°F+)
- **Misting System** for propagation areas
- **Temperature Monitoring** with alarms (prevent crop loss)

**Target Temperatures:**
- **Spring/Fall Production:** 60-75°F (achievable with minimal cooling)
- **Summer Production:** 75-85°F (requires active cooling)
- **Seedling Propagation:** 65-75°F (use misting + shade)

**Operational Strategy:**
- **Summer:** Reduce production to heat-tolerant species only (vinca, celosia, zinnias)
- **Spring/Fall:** Maximum production (pansies, mums, cool-season crops)
- **Winter:** Heat is NOT a major expense in Shiner (Zone 9a, mild winters)

### 8.3 Beneficial Insect Programs in Texas Climate

**Texas A&M Research:** 3-year study on beneficial insects in hot, humid East Texas greenhouses.

#### **Key Beneficial Insects for Texas Greenhouses**

| Pest | Beneficial Predator | Release Rate | Notes |
|------|-------------------|--------------|-------|
| **Whiteflies** | Parasitic Wasps (*Encarsia formosa*) | 2-5 per plant | Non-stinging, smaller than fruit flies |
| **Aphids** | Ladybugs (*Hippodamia convergens*) | 2-10 per plant | Also eat soft-bodied pests |
| | Lacewings (*Chrysoperla* spp.) | 2-5 per plant | Larvae are voracious predators |
| **Thrips** | Predatory Mites (*Neoseiulus cucumeris*) | Apply as sachets | Tiny, effective on young thrips |
| **Spider Mites** | Predatory Mites (*Phytoseiulus persimilis*) | Apply when first detected | Fast reproduction, outcompetes pests |
| **Fungus Gnats** | Beneficial Nematodes (*Steinernema feltiae*) | Drench soil | Kills larvae in growing media |

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Framework:**

1. **Prevention:**
   - Inspect all incoming plant material
   - Quarantine new arrivals
   - Sanitation (remove plant debris)

2. **Monitoring:**
   - Yellow sticky cards (detect whiteflies, thrips, fungus gnats)
   - Visual inspection (weekly)
   - Record pest levels (threshold-based action)

3. **Biological Control:**
   - Release beneficial insects when pest populations detected
   - Maintain beneficial populations with banker plants (if applicable)

4. **Chemical Control (Last Resort):**
   - Select insecticides compatible with beneficials (avoid broad-spectrum)
   - Spot-treat only affected areas
   - Rotate modes of action (prevent resistance)

**Texas Climate Considerations:**
- **High Temperatures:** Beneficials can be stressed above 85°F; maintain cooling
- **Humidity:** High humidity favors fungal diseases (Botrytis, powdery mildew); ensure air circulation
- **Year-Round Production:** Continuous beneficial presence prevents pest explosions

**Commercial Sources:**

- Biobest (distributor network)

## 10. CONCLUSION & KEY RECOMMENDATIONS

### Summary of Critical Adaptations

**Climate Differences:**
- Shiner is **hotter, drier, and has alkaline soil** compared to Williamsburg
- Zone 9a vs. 7b-8a = 2 full zones warmer
- Requires **50-70% less water** with adapted plant palette

**Tulip Strategy:**
- Use sparingly (5,000-10,000 bulbs in high-visibility areas)
- Pre-chill 8-10 weeks; plant January; treat as annuals
- Substitute with **Texas bluebonnets** (iconic, zero pre-chill, naturalize)

**Summer Annuals:**
- Prioritize **Texas Superstar® plants** (proven performers)
- Use **Cora XDR Vinca** (disease-resistant), Angelonia, Pentas, Purslane
- Integrate **60% native perennials** by Year 3 (80% water savings)

**Trees:**
- **Texas Live Oak** (*Quercus fusiformis*) superior to Virginia species (faster trunk girth, higher drought tolerance)
- **Crape myrtles** perform BETTER in Texas than Virginia (heat-lovers)

**European Themes:**
- **French:** Use 'Phenomenal' lavender, dwarf yaupon (boxwood substitute)
- **German Alpine:** Texas Hill Country natives (blackfoot daisy, damianita, agaves)
- **English Cottage:** Fall-plant cool-season plants, transition to heat-tolerant summer equivalents
- **Italian Mediterranean:** Excellent alignment—rosemary, Italian cypress, bougainvillea, olives

**Water Conservation:**
- **Drip irrigation:** 90% efficient (vs. 65% spray)
- **Mulch:** 3-4" depth, 50-70% evaporation reduction
- **Rainwater harvesting:** 16,000 gal/year per 1,000 sq ft roof
- **Native plants:** 80% water reduction after establishment

**Greenhouse Production:**
- Grow spring/fall annuals (pansies, mums, dianthus)
- Summer: heat-tolerant species only (vinca, celosia, zinnias)
- **Cooling essential:** Wet wall + exhaust fans + shade cloth
- **Beneficial insects:** Implement IPM (ladybugs, parasitic wasps, predatory mites)

### Final Plant Palette Recommendation (Scaled to 50,000-60,000 Annual Equivalents)

**Spring (March-May):**
- 10,000 pre-chilled tulips (high-visibility European areas)
- 15,000 Texas bluebonnets (mass plantings, naturalized areas)
- 10,000 cool-season annuals (pansies, dianthus, larkspur)
- 5,000 Indian paintbrush, phlox, wildflower mix

**Summer (May-September):**
- 20,000 Texas Superstar annuals (vinca, angelonia, pentas, purslane)
- 15,000 native perennials (establishing; permanent by Year 3)
- 5,000 heat-tolerant tropicals (SunPatiens, coleus, begonias for shade)

**Fall (September-November):**
- 15,000 chrysanthemums (heat-tolerant cultivars)
- 10,000 ornamental grasses (Gulf muhly, little bluestem—permanent)
- 5,000 fall annuals (pansies, ornamental kale)

**Trees/Shrubs (Permanent):**
- 200+ Texas Live Oaks (primary structure)
- 650+ Crape Myrtles (matching BGW density)
- 300+ native evergreens (junipers, wax myrtle, yaupon holly)
- 500+ native accent trees (redbuds, Mexican plum, desert willow)

**Total Plant Impact:**
- **Year 1:** 40,000 annuals + 20,000 establishing perennials/grasses
- **Year 3+:** 30,000 annuals (rotation) + 30,000 established perennials (permanent color)
- **Water Savings:** 50-60% vs. traditional BGW approach
- **Maintenance Savings:** 30-40% labor reduction (fewer changeouts)
