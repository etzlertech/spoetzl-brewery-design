# Landscape Project Scoping Lessons

## The Core Problem

Every landscape contractor has seen this pattern:
- Client envisions complete transformation "before summer" or "for the party in 3 weeks"
- Weather delays installation
- Plants arrive stressed from shipping
- Installation happens in 100-degree heat
- Half the plants die
- Client is unhappy, contractor reputation suffers

**This is not an execution problem. This is a scoping and timing problem.**

## The Landscaping Contractor Analogy

*(Use this with stakeholders - this is the original from FlowStar's Kai Labs lessons)*

A good landscaping contractor with a homeowner who has grand visions:

> "Why don't we start with Bed 1 — your entrance area. We get the edging, ground
> cover, shrubs, perennials, annuals, and drip irrigation all installed safely
> before spring. You enjoy something beautiful this spring while the plants
> establish in ideal weather.
>
> If we try to do all five beds plus the water fountain you just mentioned — which
> wasn't in the original request — we'd likely not get into planting until mid-summer.
> That's 100-degree days, stressful for new plants, hard to keep up with watering,
> and much less enjoyable for you.
>
> If Bed 1 goes well and you like the edging and architecture choices, we begin
> Beds 2 and 3 early fall — a better planting season. You get a chance to pivot
> on materials without a costly overhaul, especially when rocks and irrigation
> are involved."

**This prevents misalignment.** Without it: five half-finished beds, blown budget,
dead plants, no satisfaction.

## Seasonal Timing Reality (Texas Climate)

| Season | Best For | Avoid | Why |
|--------|----------|-------|-----|
| Spring (Mar-May) | Perennials, annuals, warm-season grass | Major hardscape | Unpredictable rain, mud delays concrete work |
| Summer (Jun-Aug) | Hardscape, irrigation | Planting (unless committed to intensive watering) | 100°+ temps stress new plants |
| Fall (Sep-Nov) | Trees, shrubs, perennials | Water features with pumps (freeze risk) | BEST planting season - roots establish before summer |
| Winter (Dec-Feb) | Hardscape, planning | Tropical plants, irrigation testing | Freeze damage risk, dormant season |

## What Compresses Timelines

✅ **Good conditions:**
- Spring or fall installation (60-80°F ideal)
- Client has irrigation already installed and functional
- Site is cleared and prepped (no demo required)
- Materials are in stock locally
- Weather forecast is stable for 2 weeks
- Soil is workable (not saturated, not rock-hard)
- Client decisions are made upfront (no "I'll decide when I see it")

## What Stretches Timelines

❌ **Risk factors:**
- Summer installation (requires intensive watering, plant stress risk)
- Hardscape + planting in same phase (weather-dependent sequencing)
- Custom materials (special-order stone, unique plants)
- Irrigation install + planting in same week (testing delays)
- Unknown soil conditions (could require amendment, drainage work)
- Trees/large specimens (delivery scheduling, crane access)
- Water features (leak testing, pump troubleshooting adds 3-5 days)
- Permitting required (city approvals add 2-4 weeks minimum)
- Client wants to "see it first" before approving plants (adds 1 week per decision)

## Project Category Calibration

Use these baselines for Spoetzl-scale projects:

| Category | Scope | Typical Timeline | Weather Risk | Best Season |
|----------|-------|-----------------|--------------|-------------|
| Garden Bed Installation | Single bed, plants + edging + mulch | 2-3 days | Medium | Fall/Spring |
| Multi-Bed Enhancement | 3-5 beds, irrigation, edging | 2-3 weeks | High (if planting) | Fall |
| Hardscape Feature | Patio, walkway, retaining wall | 3-6 weeks | Low (rain delays concrete) | Summer/Winter |
| Water Feature | Fountain, pond, pondless waterfall | 4-8 weeks | High (leak testing, pump issues) | Spring (before heat) |
| Full Zone Transformation | Multiple elements, irrigation, hardscape, planting | 8-16 weeks | Very High | Phased: hardscape summer, plant fall |
| Mature Tree Installation | Large specimen trees (15+ gallon) | 1 week per tree | Medium (delivery logistics) | Fall/Winter |

## Red Flags to Watch For

These patterns ALWAYS mean the timeline estimate is too aggressive:

1. **"Before summer" when it's already April** — planting season closing fast
2. **"For the wedding in 6 weeks"** — no buffer for weather delays, dead plants, or rework
3. **Client adding "just one more thing"** — scope creep, especially hardscape mid-project
4. **No irrigation in place** — adds 1-2 weeks minimum, plus testing time
5. **"We'll figure out the plants later"** — indecision kills timelines
6. **Summer planting without irrigation commitment** — plants WILL die without daily watering
7. **Concrete work during spring** — rain delays are guaranteed
8. **Special order materials** — add 2-4 weeks for delivery
9. **"I want to see the plants before you install"** — adds 1 week per approval round

## The "Bed 1 First" Principle

**Always propose a phased approach:**

### Phase 1: Highest-Impact, Lowest-Risk
- Entrance area or most visible bed
- Establish design aesthetic and quality standards
- Test client approval process and material choices
- Prove out irrigation and soil conditions
- Deliver satisfaction quickly

### Phase 2: Expand on Success
- Use learnings from Phase 1
- Client has confidence in team and aesthetic
- Can pivot on materials if needed without massive rework
- Seasonal timing can be optimized

### Phase 3: Complex Additions
- Water features, large trees, custom elements
- Only after core design is established and functional
- Client has already experienced value

## Agent Swarm Scoping Approach

When evaluating a project scope, spawn these agent personas:

### 1. Season Strategist (General-Purpose Agent)
**Persona**: Master gardener focused on plant success
**Task**: Evaluate seasonal timing and plant stress risks
**Considerations**:
- Current date vs. ideal planting windows
- Weather forecast and historical patterns
- Plant establishment time before stress season

### 2. Logistics Coordinator (Plan Agent)
**Persona**: Project manager focused on sequencing
**Task**: Plan installation phases and dependencies
**Considerations**:
- Hardscape before planting
- Irrigation before plant installation
- Material lead times
- Weather buffer days

### 3. Risk Assessor (General-Purpose Agent)
**Persona**: Experienced contractor who's seen it all go wrong
**Task**: Identify timeline killers and red flags
**Considerations**:
- Permitting requirements
- Site access challenges
- Soil/drainage unknowns
- Client decision bottlenecks

### 4. Budget Reality Checker (General-Purpose Agent)
**Persona**: Estimator focused on honest costs
**Task**: Prevent budget surprises
**Considerations**:
- Material costs + 15% buffer
- Labor costs for realistic timeline
- Equipment rental needs
- Contingency for site conditions

## Example: Spoetzl Brewery Entrance Enhancement

**Client Request**: "Transform entrance area with German beer garden theming before Oktoberfest (8 weeks away, currently July)"

**Agent Swarm Assessment**:

| Agent | Finding |
|-------|---------|
| Season Strategist | 🛑 VERY HIGH RISK - Mid-summer planting in Texas heat. Plants will be severely stressed. Recommend hardscape only, defer planting to September. |
| Logistics Coordinator | Phase 1 (4 weeks): Edging, decomposed granite pathways, seating area. Phase 2 (4 weeks, starting September): Planting beds with drip irrigation. |
| Risk Assessor | Red flags: compressed timeline, summer heat, Oktoberfest hard deadline. Mitigation: focus on hardscape that creates "bones" of beer garden. Add container gardens (can be replaced if stressed). |
| Budget Reality | Hardscape phase: $15K. Planting phase: $8K. Total: $23K. Client budget: $20K. Need to reduce scope or increase budget. |

**Recommendation**:
✅ Proceed with modified scope
- **Phase 1 (Before Oktoberfest)**: Hardscape "bones" of beer garden - seating, pathways, edging, shade structures
- **Phase 2 (September-October)**: Planting beds, live plants in ideal season
- **Compromise**: Use container gardens for Oktoberfest (can be replaced), install permanent beds in fall

- No more "why didn't you tell me this would take longer?"
- Clients understand seasonal timing realities
- Phased approach prevents overcommitment
- Budget expectations are realistic
